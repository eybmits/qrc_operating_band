\title{Memory-Defined Phase Maps for Dissipative Quantum Reservoirs}
\author{Anonymous QCE Submission}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study when a stateful dissipative gate-model quantum reservoir is useful for time-series prediction. The main claim is not quantum advantage, but a reproducible phase-map claim: across tasks and seeds, good validation performance concentrates in a memory-defined operating regime with gentle input drive, nonmaximal recurrent coupling, and controlled amplitude damping. Memory capacity and intrinsic processing-capacity diagnostics identify this regime before exhaustive task sweeps. As supporting evidence, the same four-qubit, two-layer QRC96 architecture, with only ridge readout training, is competitive with an audited ESN100 baseline at matched classical readout dimension. Under shared validation selection it improves seed-level mean holdout NMSE, and under task-wise validation-plateau selection it improves the non-floor NARMA10 and Sunspots comparisons. The comparison is matched in readout features, not in measurement, shot, calibration, wall-clock, or hardware cost.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
quantum reservoir computing, dissipative dynamics, memory capacity, phase diagrams, echo-state networks

\section{Question and Claim}
Reservoir computing trains a linear readout on fixed nonlinear dynamics \cite{jaeger2004,lukosevicius2009}. In a quantum reservoir, the useful resource is not Hilbert-space dimension alone. The physical dynamics must preserve recent input history, forget old history, and expose nonlinear observables to a classical readout \cite{fujii2017,tanaka2019,sannia2024}. Excessive input drive overwrites the carried state; absent dissipation prevents controlled fading memory; absent recurrent mixing leaves weak temporal features.

This paper therefore treats the phase diagram as the scientific object. We ask whether a dissipative QRC has a transferable operating regime, rather than whether one tuned coordinate wins one benchmark. The main result is that phase maps generalize as a frequency-level regime across four tasks and ten reservoir seeds. Accuracy against ESN100 is a supporting claim: it tests whether features from this regime are useful at a comparable classical readout dimension.

\section{Model and Selection Protocol}
The reservoir carries a density matrix from one input time step to the next. For scalar input $u_t$,
\begin{equation}
\rho_{t,0}=U_{\mathrm{in}}(u_t)\rho_{t-1,L}U_{\mathrm{in}}^\dagger(u_t),
\qquad
U_{\mathrm{in}}(u_t)=\bigotimes_i R_y(\beta u_t).
\end{equation}
Each virtual layer applies frozen local $R_x$ rotations, a ZZ ring, and product amplitude damping:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_{t,\ell}
&=\mathcal{D}_{\gamma}^{\otimes n}\!\left[
U_\lambda U_{\rm mix}\rho_{t,\ell-1}
U_{\rm mix}^\dagger U_\lambda^\dagger\right],\\
U_\lambda
&=\prod_{(i,j)\in E_{\rm ring}}e^{-i\lambda Z_iZ_j}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The QRC96 architecture is fixed throughout the ESN comparison: $n=4$, two layers, ring topology, $R_x$--ZZ--$R_x$ mixer, uniform input, amplitude damping, and ridge readout only. QRC96 measures 12 local Pauli expectations and 36 ring-pair Pauli correlations per layer, giving 96 readout features.

For task $j$ and seed $s$, let $r_{j,s}(\theta)$ be the validation percentile rank of $\theta=(\beta,\lambda,\gamma)$, with lower rank better. A transferable operating band is
\begin{equation}
B_{p,q}=\{\theta:\Pr_{j,s}[r_{j,s}(\theta)\le p]\ge q\}.
\end{equation}
This frequency-level definition avoids overclaiming a universal optimum. The strict all-seed top-20\% intersection is empty, but $B_{20,0.7}$ contains four connected points and $B_{20,0.6}$ contains fourteen.

\section{Evidence}
\textbf{Phase-map transfer.} Fig.~\ref{fig:phase} shows the core observation: low validation error is not scattered randomly across the grid. It concentrates in a small-input, nonzero-damping, nonmaximal-coupling region, and the leave-one-task-out maps preserve the same shape. The leave-one-task-out held-out validation rank is $0.240$ with bootstrap CI $[0.218,0.271]$, so the regime is a top-quartile search prior rather than a task-specific coordinate.

\textbf{Diagnostic mechanism.} Memory capacity predicts the useful region. On the current QRC diagnostic rerun, $\MC$ has Spearman $\rho_s=-0.85$ against validation rank; $\mathrm{IPC}_{\rm mem}$, $\mathrm{IPC}_{\rm tot}$, and $\mathrm{IPC}_{\rm nonlin}$ give $-0.84$, $-0.90$, and $-0.80$. Feature variation and effective rank are weakly positive, $0.16$ and $0.15$, so raw diversity alone retains poor reservoirs. Removing the mechanism also breaks accuracy: no recurrent mixing, zero damping, or dephasing raises holdout NMSE to near one, while QRC96 inside the regime is $0.077$.

\textbf{ESN comparison as supporting evidence.} Under shared validation selection, QRC96 selects $(\beta/\pi,\lambda/\pi,\gamma)=(\QRCNinetySixBetaPi,\QRCNinetySixLambdaPi,\QRCNinetySixGamma)$ and ESN100 selects $(\rho,\mathrm{input},\mathrm{leak})=(\ESNHundredSpectralRadius,\ESNHundredInputScale,\ESNHundredLeak)$. Mean holdout NMSE is \QRCNinetySixMeanTest{} for QRC96 and \ESNHundredMeanTest{} for ESN100. The paired seed-level delta $\Delta=\NMSE_{\rm ESN100}-\NMSE_{\rm QRC96}$ is \QRCSeedDeltaMean{} with CI $[\QRCSeedDeltaCILow,\QRCSeedDeltaCIHigh]$, and QRC96 wins \QRCSeedWins{} seeds. This aggregate is driven by NARMA10 under the shared selector, so we do not claim uniform task-wise dominance.

The task-wise validation-plateau selector keeps the architecture fixed but allows one setting per task for both QRC96 and ESN100. On the two non-floor tasks, QRC96 improves the combined task-seed mean by \QRCTaskwiseNonFloorDeltaMean{} with CI $[\QRCTaskwiseNonFloorCILow,\QRCTaskwiseNonFloorCIHigh]$ and wins \QRCTaskwiseNonFloorWins{} pairs. NARMA10 gives $\Delta=\QRCTaskwiseNarmaDelta{}$ with \QRCTaskwiseNarmaWins{} wins. Sunspots gives $\Delta=\QRCTaskwiseSunDelta{}$, CI $[\QRCTaskwiseSunCILow,\QRCTaskwiseSunCIHigh]$, \QRCTaskwiseSunWins{} wins, and Wilcoxon $p=\QRCTaskwiseSunWilcoxonGreaterP{}$. Mackey--Glass and Lorenz are floor checks: ESN100 is already at numerical floor and is lower there.

\section{Discussion and Scope}
The accuracy comparison is fair for a narrow benchmark statement: tasks, splits, seeds, ridge grid, holdout protocol, paired keys, and classical readout dimension are aligned; both models are selected without holdout access. It is not a full resource comparison. QRC96 requires many Pauli observables, and finite-shot sampling, measurement-basis scheduling, calibration, and hardware noise are outside this simulated study. The reviewer-safe interpretation is therefore: the phase maps identify a reproducible memory-defined operating regime; within that regime, QRC96 features are competitive with, and on NARMA10/Sunspots outperform, an audited ESN100 at matched readout dimension.

\section{Conclusion}
Dissipative QRC performance is organized by a memory-defined operating regime. Phase maps transfer across tasks and seeds, memory diagnostics predict the useful region, and ablations show that recurrent mixing plus controlled damping are necessary before richer readout access helps. The ESN comparison supports, but does not replace, the phase-map claim: at matched classical readout dimension and holdout-blind validation selection, the same QRC96 architecture gives strong aggregate accuracy and robust non-floor NARMA10/Sunspots wins. The result is not a quantum-advantage or hardware-resource claim; the next tests are finite measurements, calibration noise, and expanded classical baselines.
